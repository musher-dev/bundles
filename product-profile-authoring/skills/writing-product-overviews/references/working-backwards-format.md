# Working Backwards PR/FAQ Format

Amazon's Working Backwards method starts from the customer experience and works backward to the product. The PR/FAQ is the primary artifact — a simulated press release announcing the finished product, followed by FAQs that stress-test the idea.

## Press Release Structure

### Section-by-Section Guide

#### 1. Heading
- Product name + market category in under 10 words
- Example: "Acme Analytics — Real-Time Observability for SaaS Teams"

#### 2. Subheading
- One sentence naming the target customer and the primary benefit
- Must pass the "who + what" test: identify exactly who benefits and how
- Example: "Engineering teams can now detect and resolve incidents 80% faster with automated root cause analysis."

#### 3. Summary Paragraph (2-3 sentences)
- What is the product?
- What does it do?
- Why does it matter?
- Must be understandable by someone with zero prior context

#### 4. Problem Paragraph
- Describe the current pain point without mentioning the product
- Use concrete scenarios, not abstract statements
- The reader should nod and think "yes, I have this problem"
- Quality test: Would the target customer share this paragraph with a colleague saying "this is us"?

#### 5. Solution Paragraph
- How the product solves the problem
- Focus on mechanism (how it works) not features (what buttons it has)
- Connect directly to the problem paragraph — the solution should feel inevitable
- Quality test: Does this explain WHY the product works, not just WHAT it does?

#### 6. Stakeholder Quote
- Attributed to an internal leader (founder, VP, product lead)
- Explains why this product was built and why the timing is right
- Should convey conviction, not marketing fluff
- Quality test: Would this person actually say these words?

#### 7. Customer Experience
- Walk through a typical first interaction step by step
- Use present tense and second person ("You sign up... You see...")
- Show, don't tell — describe what happens, not what the product "enables"
- Quality test: Could a designer build a prototype from this description?

#### 8. Customer Quote
- Attributed to a real or representative customer
- Describes the outcome they achieved, not the features they used
- Includes a specific metric or result when possible
- Quality test: Is this about the customer's success, not the product's capabilities?

#### 9. Call to Action
- One clear, specific next step
- Match to product lifecycle:
  - Discovery/development: "Sign up for the beta waitlist"
  - Beta: "Request early access"
  - GA: "Start your free trial at [url]"
  - Enterprise: "Schedule a demo"

## FAQ Structure

### External FAQ (3-5 questions)
Questions a customer would ask after reading the press release.

**Required questions:**
1. Pricing or availability question
2. Comparison to alternatives question
3. Getting started question

**Quality criteria for answers:**
- Direct and specific — no "it depends" without then providing the specific cases
- Under 100 words per answer
- Include links or references where applicable

### Internal FAQ (3-5 questions)
Questions stakeholders, executives, or team members would ask.

**Required questions:**
1. Feasibility or timeline question
2. Resource requirements question
3. Risk or dependency question

**Quality criteria for answers:**
- Honest about unknowns — "We don't yet know X, and plan to learn by Y"
- Quantified where possible (cost, timeline, team size)
- Acknowledge trade-offs explicitly

## Overall Quality Criteria

| Criterion | Test |
|---|---|
| **Customer-centricity** | Is every section written from the customer's perspective? |
| **Specificity** | Could you remove any weasel words ("various", "robust", "seamless") without losing meaning? |
| **Completeness** | Would a new team member understand the product after reading this? |
| **Honesty** | Are there any claims that can't be backed by evidence? |
| **Conciseness** | Is the entire document under 2 pages (roughly 1000 words)? |
| **Coherence** | Does each section flow naturally into the next? |
